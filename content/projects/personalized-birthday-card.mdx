## Problem

I wanted to create personalized birthday cards that felt like building a tiny celebration, not filling out a plain form.

## Approach

I built the app in phases: scaffold, core builder, personalization, export, and polish. I used Codex as a coding assistant while directing the product, reviewing every output in the browser, and approving every commit. I also kept a detailed project journal documenting each phase so the process stayed intentional instead of turning into random feature-chasing.

## Key features

The builder includes four card templates: soft/sweet, confetti, pop art, and photo-focused. Users can upload a photo, move decorations around, drag the photo, stickers, and tag, choose preset messages or write custom ones, and export the finished card as a PNG.

It also includes a five-step guided wizard, native share API support with a fallback, desktop corner resize handles, and mobile pinch-to-resize.

## Tech decisions

The two-panel layout, with controls on one side and a live preview on the other, defined the whole interaction model early. It made the app feel like a builder instead of a form.

The guided wizard was a reorganization of existing controls, not a brand-new feature set. That became the biggest UX improvement: the app already worked, but restructuring the flow made it feel much easier to use.

## What I'd improve

I would add an envelope opening animation, confetti or balloon animations on reveal, and shareable links for cards that do not rely on uploaded photos.

## What I learned

This project taught me product thinking as much as React syntax. AI assistance still requires human judgment: deciding what the app should become, catching what does not feel right, and keeping the experience improving is work that does not get delegated.
